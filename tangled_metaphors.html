Tangled Metaphors

Network Thinking and Network Analysis in the History of Art

Matthew D. Lincoln, 2020

Possible use of network analysis in art history

  • flow of technical know-how and academic theory
  • connections between artists, patrons and viewers
  • trade of material goods, history of collecting
  • historiography to structure narratives about the spread of methodologies

Computational network analysis can further empower network thinking

  • if the scale of evidence goes beyond human memory
  • for measuring multiple interdependent relationships (method of network mathematics)


"The measurements one can make of a network transcend the mere sum of its individual constituents and their interconnections."

2. From Network Thinking to Network Computing

  • metrics for claryfing roles and importance of individual nodes and edges
  • metrics that characterize properties: number of connections, centrality, proximity or peripherality, key position
  • whole network metrics like centralization or diffusion, degree of distribution, density, number of connections versus number of constituents, transitivity, clusters

Computational network analysis can further empower network thinking.

Some projects:

  • Pamela Fletcher and Anne Helmreich, with David Israel, Seth Erickson: Local/Global: Mapping Nineteenth-Century London's Art Market, 2012.

  • Marten Jan Bok and Harm Nijboer: ECARTICO: Linking Cultural Industries in the Early Modern Low Countries, c. 1475-1725, 2014.

4. Limitations

"Network analyses are constrained by how one chooses to define the nodes belonging to the network and the edges that interconnect them."

Possible errors:

  • skewed: correspondance of one person with many others
  • less suitable for examining marginalized groups: not enough metadata, digitized corpora

How to overcome an error:

  • Michelle Moravec: Network Analysis and Feminist Artists, November 2017.



5. Conclusion: Networking Creatively

"...every trait of a thing is both a cluster of subordinate traits as well as a subordinate part of another cluster." (Kubler)

Though not all art-historical questions ought to be expressed as structured data, network analysis is a bridge between micro-scales and macro scales: constructed about individual people, objects, actions, ideas. At the same time, these approaches also require practitioners to be particularly precise in their assumptions, hypotheses, and descriptions of evidence, making more explicit the process of humanistic modeling that art historians are already doing.
